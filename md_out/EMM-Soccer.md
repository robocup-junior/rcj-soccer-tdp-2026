## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We designed all mechanical components with SolidWorks. We adopted a 4-camera and lidar fusion solution for football recognition and positioning, with a special wheel layout of 90° rear wheels and 120° front wheels.

During the design phase, we fully considered structural strength and manufacturing cost. We matched different materials including PLA, PETG and steel to achieve the best balance. We also arranged all cables neatly on both sides of the chassis to ensure convenient daily maintenance.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

We primarily use the Bambu Lab X1C 3D printer to manufacture most of the components of the robot.

Our PCBs are manufactured by JLCPCB.

The gears and skeleton structure are produced by an external company through metal 3D printing.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

We fully designed the mechanical structure of our  kicker in SolidWorks. The whole assembly consists of a U-shaped outer frame, a bottom cover, a central coil bobbin, copper winding coils, a moving core, a static core and an ejection push rod.

## Dribbler Design

We designed the whole dribbler mechanical structure in SolidWorks.

## Mechanical Innovation

*Mechanical Innovation*

In the design of robotic mechanical systems, our proudest innovation is the reverse hub-motor embedding design.

After finalizing the motor and kicker components, the wheel design faced a critical challenge: the motor we selected has a long shaft with an outer-biased fixing position. If this conventional scheme were followed, it would cause dimensional overrun.

we shift the wheel's fastening position from the inner side to the outer side, moving the wheel inward toward the motor.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

The robot’s main power source is a 6S high-discharge lithium battery with a capacity of 1450mAh:
1. 12V step-down circuit exclusively supplies power to the dribbler motor.

2.  5V regulator circuit power for all 5V-rated components, including MaixCam, Lidar, and Teensy 4.1 MCU.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

- Our MCU is Teensy 4.1 with native CAN peripheral. It connects to a 3.3V-powered Adafruit CAN Pal transceiver.
- The module contains an internal boost circuit to output standard 5V CAN bus level for chassis motor ESCs.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

We choose PJRC Teensy 4.1 as our main controller because its 600 MHz Cortex-M7 high-speed processor provides powerful computing performance for real-time processing tasks; it features 8 hardware UART ports, abundant GPIO pins and native CAN interface, which can connect our camera, radar, gyroscope, OLED and motor CAN bus simultaneously without extra expansion chips, fully meeting the robot’s multi-module control requirements.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

Teensy 4.1 collects data from sensor. Combined with our match strategy, it calculates target speed, moving direction and angular velocity of the  robot.

We substitute the parameters into equations. MCU conducts trigonometric solving to split the overall movement into independent target speed .

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

Four cameras mounted circumferentially (front/right/rear/left), each covers 90° with 15° overlap for 360° blind-free vision. Each runs 100Hz onboard HSV blob detection, outputs bounding box center & size via separate UARTs to Teensy 4.1. Main controller converts pixel to global angle & distance per calibrated offset, selects closest-to-center camera when multi-detected. Microswitch on dribbler senses ball contact via GPIO.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

Our robot has no line detection board. We uniquely rely solely on LiDAR distance data to determine robot position relative to field white lines for our out-of-bounds algorithm. This coordinate-based monitoring system triggers immediate corrective action when nearing restricted areas.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

For navigation, we use YDLIDAR T Mini Plus for field coordinates and HWT101 gyroscope for heading, both via serial ports to Teensy 4.1. Four onboard cameras detect goal frame position for aiming before shooting. Gyroscope provides real-time attitude feedback, enabling directional adjustments based on match strategy.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

Our 24V electromagnetic kicker uses MOT1112T MOSFET with push-pull gate drive for fast switching. Optocoupler isolates Teensy 4.1 from high-power loop. Schottky diode absorbs reverse EMF to protect MOSFET. Aluminum substrate dissipates heat; series fuse cuts power on short circuit.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

Our dribbler uses a brushless motor with ESC, controlled by Teensy 4.1 PWM for speed regulation to hold the ball. A microswitch on the frame detects ball possession; Teensy reads its logic level to confirm clamping state in real time.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

We are most proud of our custom low‑voltage high‑current electromagnet kicking system.
 
We started by testing and rewinding commercial electromagnets and found their performance could be greatly improved. From experiments, we concluded a key design trade-off: more wire turns increase magnetic force but raise resistance and lower current, which suits high-voltage driving. Under competition voltage limits, a low-voltage large-current design became our optimal development direction.
 
A 48V capacitor discharge scheme was unsuitable for our robot. If we wanted to reach 48V while maintaining high output current, we would need extremely large capacitors that cannot fit inside our limited chassis space. Direct drive with the original 6S battery voltage works much better. Batteries carry ample energy and provide strong instantaneous power output, so we specially chose high-rate lithium cells for this system. We also calculated the total power consumption of all other robot modules to allocate enough instantaneous current for the electromagnet.
 
Within a fixed coil volume, we adopted rectangular flat copper wire. Unlike regular round wire, flat wire improves space utilization, allows more winding turns and strengthens magnetic flux. We also replaced ordinary iron with DT4C high-purity soft iron for the core to achieve faster magnetization and lower hysteresis loss.
 
Mechanically, we proposed a pre-travel acceleration optimization: letting the armature move through a short accelerating stroke before striking the ball produces a faster kick. We verified this improvement with our self-built laser speed measurement device.
 
On the drive circuit side, we selected high-current MOSFETs to match our power demand, yet these chips come with a large parasitic input capacitance of 14000 pF. In comparison, small-signal low-current MOSFETs only have parasitic capacitance of several tens of picofarads. Without a high-power dedicated driver, the gate voltage VGS would take far too long to rise, slowing down the electromagnet’s response severely. To solve this issue, we designed a push-pull drive circuit to accelerate gate charging and switching speed.
 
After multiple design iterations and trial-and-error tests, our first self-made electromagnet solution is fully functional and ready for practical use. However, we have several potential improvement ideas that remain untested. We will carry out further experiments to verify these optimization directions in future work.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

Every 10ms, Teensy 4.1 reads ball bounding box data from 4 cameras via UART, computes pixel center. Local angle = (horizontal center - 224)/224 × 45°, then adds 90°/180°/270° per camera for global angle, normalized to 0-360°. Distance = vertical center/6.24 minus per-camera shift offset; clamp ≤0 to 1. Selects camera with smallest angular offset as final position. Microswitch confirms ball grip.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

Our team assigns strikers and defenders with separate algorithms. Strikers use two modes:

 (1) Tangential orbit approach—solves right triangle using ball distance as hypotenuse and virtual circle radius as leg to compute tangent offset. 

(2) Direct head-on tracking—locks robot facing ball with speed scaled by distance. 

The defender stopped on the line connecting the goal and the ball.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

LiDAR scans field, filters noise, then runs horizontal/vertical voting to identify  boundary lines with highest scores. From these lines, algorithm computes robot's X-Y absolute coordinates and heading angle for full-field localization. When no ball detected, robot returns to standby positions. When ball secured, robot combines coordinates with LiDAR data to follow fixed attacking trajectories.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

Our OOB algorithm uses LiDAR  data plus velocity decomposition. Radar scans boundaries to establish real-time coordinate vectors. For straight sidelines, decompose velocity into tangential + normal, cancel outward normal component. For curved penalty areas, cancel radial component pushing outward. Recombine remaining vectors to keep robot in-bounds while preserving motion toward ball.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

Goal-scoring: After ball capture, robot uses radar for goal angle + gyroscope for orientation. When aligned within tolerance, triggers kicker for direct shot. Alternative: radar-guided waypoint navigation along sideline to opponent goal line, then aligns and shoots. Both strategies confirm alignment via radar/gyroscope before kicker activation.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

Defense uses velocity decomposition to circle penalty zone. Radar adjusts radial velocity to maintain circular path, facing outward. Splits ball-directed velocity into components to control goal distance, recombines for precision. Positions on ball-goal line to block; stops when aligned. Clears stationary ball by turning and shooting toward opponent goal.
For an picture, please refer to the doc .

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

Two robots communicate via HC-05 Bluetooth) with Teensy 4.1 over uart, exchanging XY coordinates and strategy flags. 
Benefits:
 (1) Avoid collisions—share positions to attenuate boundary-directed vectors when close.
 (2) Eliminate ball conflicts—assign ball priority in penalty zone; non-priority robot avoids area.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

We achieve LiDAR-based full-field localization: point cloud data fused with gyroscope for correction, horizontal/vertical voting extracts top 4 line segments as field boundaries. LiDAR also detects enemy robot coordinates. Two robots share status via Bluetooth; filtering out teammate point cloud interference ensures stable enemy localization.
