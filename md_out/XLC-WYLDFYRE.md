## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We designed the robot in Fusion 360. Our main goal was to minimize weight while improving our previous robot design. We also tested different materials for the plow to find the best balance of durability and performance.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

We used 3D printers and silicone molds. Our PCBs were made by an external company from our own designs. We replaced the silicone dribbler with printer rubber rollers and refined the wheels and PCB layout for better performance.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

We used a total of 5 motors. Four motors are used for movement, allowing the robot to move in all directions. One additional motor is used for the dribbler.

We designed and manufactured our own wheels to achieve better grip, make them easy to produce in-house, and customize their size to best fit our robot's design and performance requirements.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

Our robot uses a 6 V solenoid as the kicker mechanism. To achieve the required kicking power, the solenoid is powered by a 40 V boost converter.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

The dribbler uses a MAXON motor with GT2 pulleys driving a rubber roller. The mechanism is hinge-mounted and spring-loaded. When the ball enters, it lifts the dribbler, increasing pressure. A built-in switch detects the ball by being released when the ball is captured.

*Mechanical Innovation*

This was achieved by using a lightweight 4S LiHV battery, which is about half the weight of an equivalent LiPo battery, lightweight motors, and using two PCBs as the main structural frame of the robot.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

A 4S LiHV battery (14.4–17.4V) powers the drive motors. A boost converter generates 40V for the kicker, a buck converter provides 5V for LEDs and cameras, and a 3.3V LDO powers the microcontroller and sensors.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

We use MAXON ECX motors driven by ESCON 24/2 motor drivers. The motor drivers are controlled by the microcontroller using a digital direction pin, while the motor speed is regulated with a PWM signal.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

We use an ESP32-S3 microcontroller. We chose it because of its built-in Wi-Fi and ESP-NOW protocol, which enables fast communication between our robots. It also provides high performance and is easy to program and integrate into our system.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

We use high-level functions to calculate the robot's movement vector, which is stored in a motion buffer. At the end of each control cycle, low-level functions convert it into PWM values and directions for all four motors. These signals are then sent directly to the motor drivers.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

We use an IR ball sensor that provides the ball distance, angle, and raw values, which we process in software. Each robot has two cameras, both detecting both goals for field orientation. The front camera is mainly used for shooting, while the rear camera helps the defender with positioning and navigation.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

We use 32 LED/phototransistor pairs in an outer ring and 16 in an inner cross. Reflected light from the white line changes the phototransistor voltage. Three multiplexers read all sensors. The ring detects line angle, the cross detects line depth.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

We use a BNO055 gyroscope for heading and PixyCam v2.1 cameras for field navigation by detecting both goals. All sensors are connected to the ESP32-S3 via the I²C bus.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

The microcontroller controls a MOSFET driver, which switches a MOSFET to power the kicker solenoid. A flyback circuit protects the MOSFET when the solenoid is turned off.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

The dribbler is driven by a MAXON motor controlled by a MAXON ESCON 24/2 motor driver. The motor speed is regulated by the microcontroller using a PWM signal.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

We detect the ball using an IR ball detection sensor. The sensor provides the ball direction, distance, and raw values. All data is read via I²C and processed by the microcontroller.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

The ball-catching algorithm depends on the robot's role. The defender stays near the goal line to protect the goal, while the attacker moves based on the angle between the robot and the ball. In general, both robots try to approach the ball with the front of the robot while minimizing rotation, as this gives us the best results.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

The attacker does not need an exact field position while searching for the ball. When it has the ball, it uses the cameras to detect both goals, move toward the opponent's goal, and find the best shooting position. The defender mainly uses the rear camera to detect and position itself in front of its own goal.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

We use 48 pairs of addressable LEDs and phototransistors for line detection. When a line is detected, the robot moves 180° away from it. The escape speed depends on how far the line is under the robot, measured by the inner cross of 18 LED/phototransistor pairs.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

When the attacker has the ball and sees the goal, it moves toward it, aligns to maximize the shooting angle, and shoots. The dribbler keeps the ball close to the robot for controlled movement. Once the robot has the ball, a clear view, and a good shooting angle, the kicker sends the ball into the goal.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

The defender stays near the goal area and always faces the ball to reduce the opponent's shooting angle and cover as much of the goal as possible. The attacker focuses on chasing the ball, which also helps disrupt the opponent and makes scoring more difficult.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

Yes, our robots communicate using ESP-NOW. They mainly use this communication to switch roles. When both robots are on the field, one acts as the defender and the other as the attacker, and they can swap roles to avoid blocking each other and improve teamwork.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

We are most proud of the communication between our robots. It allows them to switch roles dynamically, improving coordination, field awareness, and teamwork. This makes our gameplay more flexible and increases our chances of winning.
