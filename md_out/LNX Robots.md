## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We used Fusion 360 to make our design and Eagle for our PCBs.
Firstly, we created a 3D model of the part in Fusion 360. We have our whole robot designed 
there so it's pretty easy adding new things to our robot since the rest of it is already 
precisely modeled. Later we got them made from aluminum or we 3D printed them 
ourselves.  
There are countless hours and versions behind the way our robots look now from material 
selections to the actual parts placements.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

To build our design, we began by 3D printing all parts of the chassis.
Plastic components were printed in-house using PET-G filament.
For the 360-degree mirror, we worked with a local company that used vacuum forming with 
laminated polystyrene.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

We have five motors. 
Four motors are used for movement, these are the “Maxon EC45 flat 70W”.
We also made our own omni wheels, as we needed 6cm diameter ones and weren’t able to find any suitable on the market. 
Each omni wheel consists of two 3D-printed halves, a circular iron wire axle (1 mm thick), 
and 24 small idler rollers.
One motor is used as dribbler.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

Our kicker is a high current solenoid which we designed ourselves. To save space we also 
made it a flat solenoid as it’s commonly possible to see in the Small Size League (SSL).  
We then designed the plunger of the solenoid consisting of non-magnetic part 
and magnetic part which we got machined.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

Then there are 2 options for a point mounted dribblers high mounted and bottom mounted.
The bottom mounted were found superior since they didn't push out the ball before 
getting into the dribbling position.

## Mechanical Innovation

*Mechanical Innovation*

We try not to overcomplicate our hardware since we prioritize consistent design over 
flashy ones. However, we had at least 7 cable breakages on our maxon motors last year 
since they are brushless with encoders and each has around 20 cables. We managed to 
create our own pcb that is directly soldered onto the motor and has board-to-board 
connectors coming from it ensuring a stable and rigid connection. Also we are really proud 
of our dribbler, we only made it wider to capture the ball easier.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

Our power system uses two 3S LiPo batteries in series (22.8 V), which supplies power to our motor drivers and motors.
We use a 5 V, 5 A buck converter to 5 V for the Raspberry Pi 5. From there, we regulate the voltage to 3.3 V to power our STM32.
For the kicker, we employ a 47.5 V boost converter.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

We drive our motors using Maxon ESCON 24/2 motor drivers. Each motor is equipped with an encoder that provides position feedback at a resolution of 1024 counts per revolution for accurate speed and position control. 
The ESCON motor drivers receive PWM control signals from our STM32.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

Our robot uses a Raspberry Pi 5 with a Hailo-8L AI accelerator and two 32-bit STM32 microcontrollers. The Raspberry Pi runs vision, communication, and high-level control, using its multi-core CPU for parallel processing. The STM32F767 handles time-critical tasks such as motor control, kicker, IMU, and user interface, while the STM32G474 processes line sensor and LiDAR data. We chose STM32 over Arduino for its higher performance, richer peripherals, and industrial relevance.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The Raspberry Pi 5 calculates movement direction, speed, and rotation from sensor and sends it to the STM32, which generates PWM signals for four ESCON motor drivers with encoder feedback. The dribbler runs at a constant speed and stops briefly during kicks.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

Our vision system uses two cameras, both connected to the Raspberry Pi 5. One camera 
faces forward and is used for precise ball detection in front of the robot. The second 
camera is angled upward toward an omnidirectional mirror.
We access the camera feeds using the Picamera 2 interface.
The front-facing camera stream is processed by the Hailo-8L AI.
The mirror camera is currently processed using traditional computer vision methods.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

At the bottom of our robot, we have 8 IR sensors with IR LEDs next to them. LEDs light up 
the field and the IR sensors measure how much light reflects back. White lines reflect 
more light than the green field, so they will have a higher value.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

We use the LD19 LiDAR to determine the robot’s position on the field. It provides a 2D point cloud that we process to detect the field walls and calculate our location.
The LiDAR is connected via UART to the bottom STM32 (STM32G474), which handles 
sensor preprocessing. The IMU is also connected over UART, but to the top STM32 
(STM32F767), which manages motion control and orientation tracking.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

Our kicker uses a 47.5 V boost converter to charge 4.4 mF capacitors. Charging current is limited with two high-power SMD resistors in parallel to prevent diode damage. A gate driver switches a MOSFET from a 15 V supply for a fast 28 A kick lasting 10 ms, protecting the solenoid and circuit.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

Our dribbler uses a high-RPM motor, typically running at 4,000 RPM, to drive a silicone-covered bar. The bottom-mounted design lets the mechanism move back, maintaining ball contact. We use A60 Shore silicone, cast in a 3D-printed mold around the dribbler rod for optimal grip.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

The Raspberry Pi 5 captures images using Picamera2. The front camera uses a Hailo-8L with a custom neural network to detect the ball, while the mirror camera uses OpenCV color detection. From the ball's bounding box, we estimate distance using the pinhole camera model and calculate its angle from the image center. For the mirror camera, angle is obtained by linear interpolation.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

Our robots use atacker or goalkeeper mode. The attacker catches the ball in two ways: for balls near the sides or back, it rotates toward the ball and drives straight to it. For balls in the center, it stays facing the opponent’s goal and moves around the ball if needed before catching it. The goalkeeper only approaches the ball when it is very close, otherwise it remains in a defensive position.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

We use a LiDAR sensor to determine our robot’s position on the field, it gives us distances ang angles. We then apply a Hough transform to detect the field 
walls and estimate the robot’s exact position.
Once the current position is known, we calculate the direction and distance to the target 
location, where our robot will go.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

We use LiDAR and IR sensors to detect lines and obstacles. The LiDAR sensor allows us to 
detect walls or obstacles in advance, enabling the system to decelerate smoothly before 
getting too close – this helps prevent the robot from crossing the line too quickly, which 
could cause the IR sensors to miss it.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

After catching the ball, the attacker rotates toward the center of the opponent’s goal based on its field position and kicks. If it is near an opponent’s corner, it first moves slightly away until it has a clear shot. When catching the ball near a sideline, it dribbles along the line toward the opponent’s corner before turning and shooting.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

The goalkeeper stays between the ball (or opponent) and the goal using camera and LiDAR. If the ball is lost, it uses shared or tracked positions. LiDAR identifies opponents by excluding its teammate. When close to the ball, it attempts a kickoff; otherwise, it moves to the best blocking position, switching to a tighter defensive path when the ball is near the goal.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

Our robots communicate with each other using Bluetooth. They share key information 
such as the position of the ball, their own current positions on the field, and use this data 
to coordinate roles, dynamically deciding which robot should act as the striker and which 
as the goalkeeper.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

Our opponent detection algorithm processes LiDAR data by removing known objects such as field walls, goals, and our own robots (including teammate). The remaining points are clustered and treated as opponent positions. This allows detection of enemy robots without vision. The goalkeeper uses it to track opponents even when the ball is not visible, while the attacker uses it for basic avoidance during movement and shooting.
