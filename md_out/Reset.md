## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

Our previous dribbler (ball intake) used a gear-driven system. However, when it jammed or stalled, it could put excessive stress on the motor and potentially damage both the motor and the battery.

To improve reliability, we changed the design from gears to a synchronous timing belt system. In the event of a jam, the belt can slip or become loose, which helps absorb the overload instead of transferring the force back into the motor. This protects both the motor and the battery from damage.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

We used a 3D printer (BambuLab A1 Mini) and CNC machine (Makera Carvera) to make Fusion 360 parts. Some parts were outsourced for higher quality, and PCB designs were manufactured by JLCPCB. Small 3D-printed parts were reprinted during testing.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

We used 4 motors for the robot’s movement with omni wheels in a diagonal configuration. We chose a 4-motor setup because it is a common configuration in competition robots, as it offers a good amount of stability and torque. The use of omni wheels enables the robot to move smoothly in any direction without needing to rotate first.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

We found that thicker copper produced more power, but it also increased current and made the PCB prone to damage. We chose a copper thickness thicker than the original but not excessive for a balance between power and reliability. We also reduced solenoid power for safety.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

The dribbler uses a brushless motor connected to a silicone roller through a timing belt. The shield was CNC machined instead of 3D printed, improving strength and durability. We also used separate silicone sections, improving the dribbler’s stability.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

Our robot uses a 12V battery system. The motor drivers and ESC run directly from the 12V rail. A buck converter steps the voltage down for the microcontroller, while sensors and the IMU operate from a regulated 3.3V rail.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

The robot uses four DC motors, each driven by a DRV887x H-bridge motor driver. The Teensy controls the drivers using a phase-and-enable setup, sending PWM signals for speed control and direction signals to reverse motor rotation.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

We use a Teensy 4.1 as the main controller. We chose it for its fast processing speed, reliable PWM outputs, and real-time performance. It can simultaneously handle motor control, sensor processing, and communication, helping keep the robot responsive during operation.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The Teensy 4.1 controls four DRV887x motor drivers by outputting PWM signals (0–255 via analogWrite) for speed and digital signals for direction. Each motor is assigned specific pins, and values from kinematic calculations are continuously updated to control omni-directional movement.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

Our robot detects the ball using an OpenMV camera running a custom vision script. It tracks objects in the Lab color space and uses calibrated thresholds to identify the orange ball, as well as yellow and blue goals. It calculates the target’s position relative to the image center to determine direction and approximate distance. This data is then sent to the Teensy 4.1 through a high-speed connection, allowing the robot to continuously track the ball and react to its movement during a match.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

Our robot uses a circular line sensor board with 18 LED–GL5506 photoresistor. A co-processor reads analog inputs (A0–A17), applies a rolling average, and uses EEPROM calibration to detect the white line. Position data is sent to the Teensy 4.1, which determines line position and returns in bounds.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

Our robot uses an Adafruit BNO055 IMU and an OpenMV camera for navigation, plus 3 LiDAR sensors for distance. The IMU connects to the Teensy 4.1 via I2C (100 Hz) to track heading. The OpenMV camera sends ball/goal positions via serial. Position is estimated by combining IMU heading with camera angles for field navigation.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

The kicker uses a high-current solenoid driven by a BTH500301 PROFET high-side switch. The Teensy 4.1 triggers it via digital pin 37, sending a 70 ms pulse to fire the solenoid. Built-in protection handles faults, and software enforces a 3s cooldown between kicks.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

The dribbler uses a brushless motor driven by an ESC controlled by the Teensy 4.1 via PWM. During a match, the teensy adjusts dribbler speed based on the robot state: high speed when chasing the ball to pull and hold it against the chassis, and slower when preparing to shoot to maintain possession.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

The part of our electronics that we are most proud of is our custom dual-PCB. Instead of placing every component on a single board, we designed two specialized PCBs: a main controller board and a circular bottom sensor board. One challenge we faced was that high-power devices such as the motor drivers, dribbler ESC, and kicker could introduce electrical noise that interferes with sensitive sensors. To solve this, we placed all 18 line sensors and their dedicated co-processor on the bottom board, allowing the sensor system to process readings locally before sending the data back to the main Teensy controller. This reduced wiring, simplified the PCB layout, and helped improve the reliability of our line detection system. On the main PCB, we also moved the motor drivers and kicker control circuitry directly onto the board, which reduced exposed wiring and made the robot easier to assemble and maintain. When the robot crosses the boundary line, the two center sensors detect the midpoint and allow the robot to move backward and recover its position. Since this was our first time designing a full main controller PCB, successfully integrating all these functions into a single board was a major achievement for our team. Overall, this design gave us a cleaner, more organized electrical system while improving both reliability and performance during competition.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

Our robot finds the ball using an OpenMV camera that detects the orange ball using calibrated color thresholds. When a valid target is detected, it calculates the ball’s position relative to the image center and sends the data via serial to the Teensy 4.1. The Teensy uses this to determine ball direction and distance in real time for gameplay response.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

Our ball-catching algorithm changes based on ball position. If the ball is in front, the robot drives straight toward it while using the dribbler to secure possession. If it is farther away, it adds a lateral offset to intercept smoothly. If close but misaligned, it loops behind the ball to re-approach toward the goal. Both robots use the same logic.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

Our robot estimates its field position by combining OpenMV camera data with BNO055 IMU heading. The IMU provides absolute orientation, while the camera gives relative positions of the ball and goals. This allows continuous tracking of field landmarks. If vision is lost, it uses the last known goal position to continue reliable navigation.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

Our robot uses a circular line sensor board with 18 GL5506 light sensors around the chassis. A co-processor reads the sensors, applies a 16-sample rolling average, and uses EEPROM calibration to detect boundary lines. When a line is detected, the center sensors locate the midpoint, and the robot moves backward to return inside the field.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

Our scoring algorithm first secures possession using the dribbler. If the ball is in front, the robot drives directly toward it. If farther away, it uses a lateral offset to intercept, and if misaligned, it moves behind the ball. Once in possession, the camera checks goal alignment and the Teensy activates the kicker when a clear shot is available.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

The robot uses a defensive routine to protect the goal line and prevent scoring. If the camera loses sight of the ball, it recenters using its own goal coordinates to stay in position. When the ball is visible, it calculates angular difference and moves laterally to block shots. If the ball stays in front for too long, it triggers a clear routine to push it out before returning to position.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

We use a greedy algorithm that selects the best immediate action based on the current game state. It evaluates available options and chooses the one with the highest short-term benefit, helping the robot make fast decisions during gameplay. This step-by-step approach reduces computation time and allows efficient decision-making using simple conditions.
