## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We designed our robot using Fusion 360 for mechanical parts and EasyEDA for PCBs. Sensor placement was based on research and testing: ultrasonic sensors were mounted on a lowered top plate for accurate wall detection, IR sensors were positioned at ball height, and the camera and mirror were placed using calculated dimensions. Future improvements include a light-gate ball detector and a curved mirror for wider vision, improving ball detection and aiming accuracy.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

PCBs were designed by us and manufactured by JLCPCB. Mirror and ultrasonic sensor mounts were designed in Fusion 360 and 3D-printed. Mounting hole and motor positioning issues were resolved by redesigning mounts and adjusting component placement.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

We used 4 Joinmax 3561 motors for each robot, controlled by 2 Cytron MDD10A motor drivers. This achieved a 4-wheel drive base, enabling rapid changes in direction & independent control of rotational motion. We used omnidirectional wheels on each motor. This allows for manoeuvrable & responsive movement, faster response in gaining possession of the ball, & preventing the opposing team from scoring.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

We designed the kicker circuit in EasyEDA. A TC4420 gate driver drives an IRF3205 MOSFET, enabling our microcontroller to switch the 11.1V, high-current solenoid. An SR360 flyback diode and parallel capacitor suppress back-EMF and electrical noise, protecting the circuit.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

Our robots use an 11.1V battery, regulated to 5V to power our primary and secondary controllers, the Raspberry Pi Pico and ESP32. The motor drivers and solenoid are powered directly by the 11.1V battery to provide sufficient current and voltage for reliable motor performance and kicking strength.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

Our robot uses dual H-bridge motor drivers to control the DC motors. The Raspberry Pi Pico sends PWM and direction signals to the drivers, allowing precise speed and direction control. The motor drivers are powered directly by the 11.1V battery for sufficient power output.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

We used a Raspberry Pi Pico as the primary controller due to its dual-core processor and PIO blocks, enabling fast real-time processing of data from the compass, camera, ultrasonic sensors and IR compound eyes for responsive navigation. An ESP32 acts as a secondary controller, continuously sampling TEMT6000 phototransistors using its ADCs for rapid white-line detection. Separating these tasks prevents processing bottlenecks and improves overall robot performance throughout matches.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The Pico is connected to the motor drivers via gpio analog. Each motor is controlled with a speed and a direction pin. For the speed pin, To drive at a certain angle, the speed of each of the motors required is calculated using trigonometry, as they are mounted at 45 degree angles around the robot.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

We used two compound eyes mounted at the front and rear of the robot to achieve 360° ball detection. They communicate via I2C using the TwoWire library. As both sensors share the same address, they are connected to separate I2C buses. Each compound eye has seven channels that report IR intensity. The strongest signal and its neighbouring channels are interpolated to calculate the ball’s angle precisely. This serves as our primary ball detection system, without relying on the camera.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

Our line detection circuit uses a TEMT6000 sensor and LED. The LED illuminates the field and the sensor measures reflected light. White lines reflect more light than the green field, producing a higher voltage. The Pico reads this analogue signal and detects the boundary with a calibrated threshold.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

We use ultrasonic sensors to determine the position of the robot on the field. To ensure that the robot is always facing the front so as to capture the ball with the ballcap, a compass is utilised. The target angle is always set to 0 degrees, and the error is calculated. This is used to rotate the robot to the front.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

A boost converter charges a capacitor from the 12 V battery. When commanded, the Pico triggers a TC4420 gate driver, switching an N-channel MOSFET to discharge the capacitor through the solenoid. This creates a magnetic field that launches the plunger to kick the ball.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

Although we used a commercially available solenoid, we designed the electronics required to integrate it into our robot reliably and efficiently. A custom driver circuit allows the microcontroller to safely control the high current required by the solenoid, while a capacitor bank stores energy and rapidly discharges it to deliver a powerful, consistent kick. The circuit was optimised to recharge quickly after each shot, enabling repeated kicks during matches. Protection components were also included to prevent voltage spikes from damaging the electronics, ensuring reliable operation throughout competitions.

We designed custom PCBs to integrate all of our robot’s electronics into a compact and reliable system. Rather than relying on jumper wires and prototype boards, each PCB was tailored to our robot’s layout and electrical requirements. Dedicated connectors for sensors, motors and communication buses simplify assembly and maintenance, while separating power and signal traces reduces electrical interference. Wider high-current traces safely handle the motor and kicker circuits, resulting in a cleaner, more robust and competition-ready electronics system.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

We used 2 compound eyes at the front & rear for 360° ball detection. They communicate via I2C with the TwoWire library. As both share the same address, they are connected to separate I2C buses. Each sensor has 7 IR channels. By interpolating the strongest signal with its adjacent channels, the robot calculates the ball angle more precisely, providing reliable tracking without camera reliance.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

Instead of driving directly at the ball, which could knock it away, our robot orbits the ball to approach from behind. Four movement cases are defined based on the ball’s position. The drive angle is calculated from the ball angle plus a scaled offset based on the maximum IR intensity, while orbit speed is also scaled using IR intensity for smoother, more reliable ball capture.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

We use 4 ultrasonic sensors to measure the robot’s distance from each wall, allowing its coordinates on the field to be calculated. These coordinates enable movement to target positions, such as the field centre or along the goal line. Using basic trigonometry, the robot calculates the required heading, while its speed is scaled according to the remaining distance for smooth, accurate navigation.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

To detect field boundaries, reflected light sensors identify the contrast between green and white surfaces. Arranged in a ring, they let the robot move away when a line is detected. Four ultrasonic sensors estimate field position by measuring wall distances. If near a wall or localisation confidence is low due to blocked sensors, the robot slows to improve navigation and accuracy.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

If the IR intensity is greater than a certain value and the ball angle is within a certain range, (e.g. 345-15 degrees), the ball is considered within the ball capture zone. The robot accelerates and faces the goal, delivering a kick to score.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

The goalie remains at the goal line (along a certain y coordinate), and follows the ball such that it is always directly in front of it. If the ball is within the ball capture zone after stopping a goal, the goalie switches to a striker and brings the ball towards the centre.

*Software Innovations*

One software innovation we are most proud of is developing the robot’s autonomous navigation and ball-tracking system. We fused data from infrared ball sensors, ultrasonic localisation, the compass, goal camera and boundary sensors.
