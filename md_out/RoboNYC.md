## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We used Fusion360 to design the 3D CAD for the shields, dribbler, and light gate for our robot, then printed them using a BambuLab printer. We implemented a dribbler this year. We also improved our design from last year by adding flexible TPU shields, which absorbs collision shocks better than our previous shields. Additionally, we experimented with the design of our ball capture zone to have a rounded yet wide enough area for the robots to control the ball effectively.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

We used KiCad to design our PCBs. We then sent these new designs to JLC PCB to manufacture them. Much of our robot's body is built of 3D printed parts. We designed these parts on Fusion 360, and then used a Bambu A1 to print them with PLA and TPU.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

We are using four 9.7:1 Pololu DC motors (Pololu item #4842) on each of our robots. We decided to stick with a four-wheel drivetrain over a three-wheel one because it provided us with good stability, weight distribution, and traction. We use 50mm omniwheels from GTFRobots. The links to these parts are in our BOM. We bought the motors with encoders attached for localization purposes.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

This year, we opted to not use a kicker due to weight constraints. Instead, we innovated a bidirectional dribbler that will launch the ball by reversing the dribbler direction, acting as a kicker. We experimented with different torques and RPM to balance grip and speed.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

Our back dribbler uses a 1000kV drone motor and a pulley system in order to capture the new smaller orange ball. We also implemented a new innovative bidirectional ESC, allowing us to use our dribbler as a kicker. We use a backspin strategy to capture the ball and flick it across the field.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

Our robot has a 12V battery that powers the 4 DC motors, dribbler drone motor, motor drivers, and 5V and 3.3V buck converters. The 5V then goes to the Teensy 4.1, IR sensors, and camera, while the 3.3V powers the light, orientation, and ultrasonic sensors. We use a MOSFET to regulate the voltage.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

We drive four motors, each through its own Pololu High-Power Simple Motor Controller G2 18v15 (#1363), commanded by the Teensy over serial. The controllers run on 12V from the battery and drive four 9.7:1 Metal Gearmotor 25Dx63L mm HP 12V motors with 48 CPR encoders (#4842).

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

We chose to use a Teensy 4.1 microcontroller because it has a large number of pins that support all of our sensors. It also supports many protocols, including I2C, which allows us to connect multiple sensors to a single port. Although it is somewhat fragile and damages more easily than other boards, we decided that the Teensy is convenient and lightweight enough to outweigh these drawbacks. Our robot only uses one processor, but we may consider more in the future for more analog ports.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The Teensy 4.1 drives our four motors over serial ports. We compute wheel speeds with sin/cos, then send speed/direction(0-3200) to four motor drivers. Those drivers generate the PWM, rapidly switching each motor's H-bridge on/off–duty cycle sets speed, a forward/reverse byte sets direction.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

Our robots use two IR compound eyes, each with seven ports, mounted on the left and right sides for full 360° vision. Each sensor returns an IR strength reading: where we take the vector sum of the top 5 ports to triangulate the ball’s position.

Our robots also carry a M5 Stack camera that differentiates objects by color including the orange ball. This tracks both the ball and our position on the field, letting us judge if the ball is in our capture zone and how our current position.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

The baseplate PCB ring connects 48 light sensors that detect white lines. Three 74HC4067 16-channel analog multiplexers combine their outputs into far fewer Teensy inputs, cutting pin count and wiring complexity. A 10-pin JST routes power and signal to the second plate, which holds the Teensy.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

Many sensors work to locate the robot. Four URM13 sensors measure the distance from the walls to find our position on the field. A BNO055 compass tracks rotation to prevent us from overturning. Light sensors keep us from crossing the bounds, and let the robot follow the line on defense. Cameras locate us relative to the goal so we can drive toward its center to score.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

The Teensy sends a PWM pulse to a bidirectional ESC powering the dribbler motor, setting direction and speed: above neutral the wheel spins one way to grip the ball and hold it. When ready, the ESC reverses below neutral, spinning the wheel the opposite way to fling the ball forward and launch it.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

Our dribbler is a drone motor driven by a bidirectional ESC powered by the 12V battery. The Teensy controls it with a PWM signal on pin 12, whose pulse width sets both speed and direction: one way grips the ball with backspin to hold it, the reverse flings it forward to launch.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

The electronics we're proudest of is our baseplate light-sensor board. Beyond the 48-sensor line-detection ring, two choices make it reliable in any venue and at any battery level: a potentiometer that tunes the array to the room's lighting, and a MOSFET circuit that regulates the voltage feeding it.

1. The potentiometer: adapting to lighting in seconds
Our 48 downward-facing sensors find the white border by reflected light: white reflects a lot, green carpet little. But ambient light varies by venue, pushing every reading too high (all white) or too low (no line). So we put a potentiometer on the PCB that sets the array's operating point. One knob shifts all 48 sensors into the usable middle of our analog-to-digital range, where green and white separate cleanly. It's an instant field adjustment, no laptop or re-flashing.

The innovation is pairing this analog trim with software calibration. The pot gets the array into its sweet spot; then we press calibrate and sweep every sensor over the green carpet. The robot records each sensor's strongest green reading, sets an individual threshold, and saves all 48 to non-volatile memory. Dead sensors are detected and locked out so they can't false-trigger. The pot handles the global offset and the firmware handles each sensor's sensitivity, staying robust without re-tuning code at every competition. (Three 16-channel chips multiplex all 48 sensors down to a few pins.)

2. The MOSFET voltage regulation: a stable rail under load
The pot setting only stays meaningful because a MOSFET circuit regulates the voltage to the sensors. Our battery isn't a stiff 12V: a LiPo sags over a match and droops hard when all four motors pull current at once. If the sensors rode that noisy motor rail, readings would drift and our start-of-match thresholds would go wrong. So we use MOSFETs to hold a clean, regulated rail, decoupled from motor power. Their very low on-resistance switches the load efficiently with little wasted heat, unlike a linear regulator. That keeps the rail constant regardless of battery state or load, so the saved thresholds stay valid the whole match. Those three layers (regulated voltage, pot trim, and per-sensor calibration) make line detection that doesn't drift as the battery drains, which is what we're most proud of.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

Two IR sensors, one on each side of the robot, give a full 360° view to spot the ball. Each port has seven IR sensors that return a strength reading. We compute the direction vector using the top x readings from the ports, where we can determine with higher accuracy compared to just taking the port with higher strength.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

Our striker chases the ball but doesn't drive straight at it: it curls along an arc, the steering offset growing as the ball sits more to its side, so it sweeps around and traps the ball facing the goal. Our goalie does the opposite. It hugs the white goal line and only slides left/right to stay between the ball and the net, never chasing into the field. The ball just picks which way it slides.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

Four ultrasonic sensors measure our distance to each wall, telling us where we sit on the field. Our airwall algorithm feeds those distances into a virtual force field: it splits our drive into x/y parts and, as a wall nears, scales that part down then reverses it. This curves the robot away from walls and the out-of-bounds line while it keeps chasing the ball.
https://youtu.be/iRhOrAF4o7Y

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

A ring of 48 light sensors spots the white line. Each sensor that sees white contributes a unit vector in its direction; we sum these into one vector pointing toward the line, clustering segments to handle corners. The robot then drives the opposite way to escape back inbounds. Our ultrasonic air wall backs this up, curving us off the walls before we reach the line.
https://youtu.be/bMGGJdDkwOE

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

Once we have the ball and we’re close to the edge of the field, we do backspin strategy. The robot backs up and spins in place to fling it toward the goal. If we have the ball and we’re in the middle of the field, our camera finds the goal by its color, locks onto its center, and we drive straight at that angle to carry the ball in and score. 
https://youtu.be/qSJhCxreypY

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

Our goalie hugs the white goal line, sliding left/right to stay between the ball and the net instead of chasing into the field. We designed and tuned this line-following algorithm with our own Python physics simulator: you drive the robot with arrow keys and it returns a target direction and speed. Anyone can run it: https://github.com/jscwang/2026-RoboNYC/tree/master/Python-Sim

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

We used an HC05 Bluetooth system to switch defense to offense when the offense robot gets turned off; We also use it to localize the position of the ball, if the defense robot can’t see the orange ball with its camera.
