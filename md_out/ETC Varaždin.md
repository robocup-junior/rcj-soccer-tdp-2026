## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We opted for a closed octagonal chassis design. Our goal was to make the chassis as compact and possible and keep the assembly of the robot easy and quick. We made the chassis to consist of as few parts as possible in order to achieve as compact of a design as possible. Components like motors, motor drivers and the kicker are all placed on the bottom part of the chassis and a designated placed in the chassis for a battery was also added.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

All chassis parts were 3D printed at our school and PCBs were ordered from JLCPCB. Parts such as screws and nuts were bought locally, and custom polyurethane wheel treads were ordered locally from Gumiimpex.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

Each robot has four Joinmax JMP-BE 3561 motors since they provide sufficient power and speed for the needs of our robots. We also designed custom wheels and opted on a dual-layer design, where each wheel consists of two basic wheels in order to reduce the gap between two rubber rollers for it to achieve better grip.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

As our kicker we use a powerful solenoid motor and have designed a custom PCB to control it. Two main parts of the PCB are a strong MOSFET and a level shifter which is needed to be able to trigger the kicker reliably with the signal from Teensy.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

Our robot operates on three voltage levels: 12V, 5V, and 3.3V. Battery is connected to our power supply PCB which provides 12V for our motor drivers and the kicker, the PCB also has two step-down voltage regulators that provide 3.3V and 5V for parts of the robot such as microcontrollers and sensors.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

For our motor drive circuit, we use four ML-R 1x50A MRMS motor drivers which are powered by 12V from our power supply PCB. Each motor has its own motor driver and is controlled using two pins connected to the micro controller; a PWM pin to manage the speed and a DIR pin to manage motor’s direction.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

We use Teensy 4.1 as our main micro controller because of its processing power and sufficient number of I/O pins as well as for its proven reliability. Teensy controls the motors and all of our robots’ sensors except the ball sensor. As our secondary micro controller, we picked XIAO ESP32-C6 since it doesn’t take up as much space while still having enough pins to connect our custom ball sensor to it, and it allows our robots to be able to communicate using the ESP now protocol.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

We have a function that receives the desired speed and movement angel. using trigonometric functions movement speed and direction are calculated for each motor. the micro controller outputs the wanted speed and direction to each of the motors using PWM pin for speed and DIR pin for direction.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

We made a custom ball sensor as a part of our main PCB that consists of 16 IR receivers aligned in a circle, they are connected to a 16-channel multiplexer which is connected to our secondary micro controller XIAO ESP-C6. Our secondary micro controller calculates the ball angle and that data is sent to our main micro controller Teensy 4.1 via UART. We also use an openMV H7+ camera facing forward so that when the ball is detected in front of the robot we are able to get robot's distance from it.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

We made a custom line detection sensor which consists of 48 ALS-PT19-315C/L177/TR8 ambient light sensors each paired with a white LED to help in harsh light conditions. the 48 sensors are connected to three 16-channel analog multiplexers which are connected to our main micro controller.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

We use four ultrasonic distance sensors to roughly estimate the robots’ position and know how far it is from the field boundaries which helps us to make a more reliable line avoidance function. Each of the four sensors is connected to our main micro controller via a single analog pin. For maintaining the direction the robot faces we use BNO055 IMU which is connected to Teensy via I2C.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

the kicker PCB receives a 3.3V signal from Teensy. the signal goes to a level shifter which outputs a 5V signal which is needed to activate the MOSFET. When activated, the MOSFET connects the kicker to 12V and triggers it. there is also a strong capacitor to allow for more powerful kicks.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

Our secondary micro controller reads all 16 channels of a multiplexer to see which sensors are detecting the ball. The ball angle is then calculated using a weighted circular average of all the sensors that saw the ball. Calibration and smoothing (EMA) is than applied to the raw angle to reduce noise and the final angle is than sent to the main micro controller via UART.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

We made a few different balls following functions for our attacker robot. The function we use the most is made so that the robot is always trying to get directly behind the ball to trigger the kicker system. Another function we use makes the robot overshoot the ball slightly on purpose in order to be able to score a goal from neutral points near the opposing goal.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

we use ultrasonic distance sensors to determine robots' distance from the white lines by detecting where the walls of the filed are. Using that info, we made a ball following function which slows the robot down as it is approaching the lines in order to ensure a reliable line detection. This way we are able to set the top movement speed much higher and not worry about line detection reliability.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

The robot uses our custom line sensor to detect the white lines. The sensors are divided into four groups (front, back, left, right) and when a line is detected the robot calculates the opposite direction from there the line was detected. to ensure reliable line detection the robot gradually slows down as it is approaching the white lines.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

We mainly rely on the kicker against teams with a goalkeeper; the robot always gets directly behind the ball and uses the camera to aim at the goal when the robot has the ball. Against teams without the goalkeeper, we use a program that is able to score goals when the ball is placed on neutral spots near the opposing goal since our robot is quite fast and often is the first to get to the ball.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

Our goalkeeper robot relies on a camera facing backwards to know the position and distance of its own goal as it is moving left or right to get in front of the ball. The robot always turns so that the back of it is always facing the goal center ensuring that it moves in a semicircle around the goal. To keep a desired distance from the goal a PID-inspired program is used to align the robot.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

Our robots are able to communicate using ESP now protocol. We tried implementing this functionality to our program but unfortunately, we weren’t able to find a useful function for it and decided that the robots work better individually without communication.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

Software innovation we are the most proud of is our addition of using distance sensors to determine the distance from the walls of the field and slowing down as the robot is approaching the white lines. By using this program, we noticed a rise in the average amount of goals the robot scores during a match and a drop in the number of times the robot goes out to bounds because of the more reliable line detection due to the robot slowing down.
