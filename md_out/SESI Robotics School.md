## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We made our design entirely in Fusion360 to make 2D and 3D models. We came up with this final design after searching for international teams strategies and mechanical structure. We faced many challenges, like the ball capturing zone (1.5 cm) and the adaptation to the new maximum weight. In comparison to the last competition, our robot now uses the TSSP4038 and the BNO055. This change demanded a new design for the TPU holders and with the creation of a similar chassis basis made in Policarbonate.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

The Kobra S1 3D printer and the Duplotech 6040 for 3D and 2D parts,using PC and TPU filament and acrylic plates. Instead of 3D printing, our omniwheels are made of aluminum by GTF Robotics. Our PCBs were designed in EasyEDA and printed by JLCPCB.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

We used 4x JGA25-370 620 RPM. At the beginning of the season we used 25GA-370, but due to the lack of RPM(280) we decided to buy a new motor. Lastly, we decided to use 4 motors per robot because by adding just 1 motor the robot can become stronger, faster and it can lead to better navigation too.

## Mechanical Innovation

*Mechanical Innovation*

The mechanical part we are most proud of is the IR PCB Support. This new support allowed us to use the TSSP4038 to its best way by using small boxes with a centered fissure enveloped with aluminium, which is responsible for limiting the angle at which the ball is read. That way, by attaching these boxes in each one of the 16 spaces in the “crown of IR”, we can have a solid read of the ball.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

Our robot uses a 11.1V battery pack which is connected to two switches in parallel. One of them activates the microcontroller, while the other turns on the motors. Before getting in the ESP32-S3, the voltage is regulated to 5V through a buck converter.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

The motors are driven through a L293D IC. Each IC is capable of controlling the speed and direction of two motors, so we use two of them to drive our four motors. Besides being powered by the battery voltage, this component is attached directly to the PCB.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

There are 4 ESP32-S3 microcontrollers. One of the processors is located in the Main PCB, responsible for controlling the motor through two L293D ICs and processing the data from BNO055 (compass). The second one is in charge of processing the data from each of the IR sensors’ readings. The third microcontroller processes the ultrasonics’ data and the last one interprets the reflectance of the field based on the fluctuation of resistance in the LDRs.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The L293D IC has 6 digital inputs, 3 responsible for controlling each motor. Two of them control the direction of the motor: if LOW/LOW or HIGH/HIGH, the motor is turned off; if HIGH/LOW or LOW HIGH, the motor activates. The third pin controls the speed of the motor, by receiving an 8-bit value.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

Our ball detection system consists of a PCB with 16 TSSP4038 IR sensors. There’s a 22.5° gap between each, organized in a round pattern. They identify the angle of the ball relative to the robot. However, we cannot read the intensity of the signal with them, so we also use a NXT Infrared Seeker for this purpose. If we didn’t, it wouldn’t be possible to know how close the ball is. As a result, our robot is able to have a ball detection system with 360° coverage and to read intensity as well.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

It consists of 5 LDRs and 4 LEDs. The light from the LEDs is reflected by the field: white reflects more light than green. The reflected light reaches the LDRs and their resistance varies. The microcontroller measures the voltage difference and confirms the presence of a line.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

We use a BNO055 connected via I2C to the Main microcontroller in order to measure the direction that the robot is facing. To find our position on the field we use 3 ultrasound sensors (US-015) located in the right, left and front.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

The part that we are most proud of is definitely our Infrared PCB. We have been effectively working on it since 2025, with a lot of attempts. In the beginning of our production, we used a NXT Infrared Seeker to detect the ball. It was easy to work with, but it had a lot of inconsistencies and even a 120° blind spot. Last year, we tried using a multiplexer to connect 16 IR sensors, but some would occasionally stop reading and it had a bit of latency. This year, we’ve abandoned the multiplexer and connected 16 TSSP4038 IR sensors directly to the ESP32-S3, in a round pattern. The data from the sensor readings is sent to the Main PCB through a wireless protocol called ESP-NOW. As a result, the Main PCB decides which way to go based on the direction of the ball.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

Our microcontroller  performs a sampling of 200 digital readings from the 16 sensors, summing the values into a variable every time a HIGH signal is detected. We then calculate the ball's direction by applying a vector sum based on the angles of the sensors and the "strength" of the detections.
The process is handled by a dedicated ESP32-S3. After that, the data is transmitted to the main board.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

For our attacker, we execute an "orbit" maneuver. It calculates the shortest trajectory that curves behind the target, placing the robot smoothly behind the ball. This is achieved by adding or subtracting the IR sensor values from the motor powers via a vector sum.
Our defender's movement is strictly lateral, maintaining a blocking position directly in front of the ball within the goal area.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

We determine the individual velocity of each motor by decomposing the desired trajectory vector. We then feed orientation data from a BNO055 compass into a Proportional-Derivative (PD) controller.This generates a correction signal that is applied to the individual motor velocities. 
Furthermore, we determine our absolute position on the field by using ultrasonic sensors.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

To prevent the striker from leaving the field, we use 3 ultrasonic sensors. When these sensors detect a distance below the set threshold, the robot maneuvers away from the wall. Furthermore, to avoid entering the goal area, we use an array of five LDRs at the front of the robot. If any of these sensors detect a line, the robot immediately moves backward.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

We rely purely on the kinematic pushing force of our attacker.
Our goal-scoring algorithm consists of 3 main objectives: verifying our field limits via ultrasonic readings, adjusting our motor power via the compass PD controller to keep the robot constantly facing the opponent's goal, and executing our orbit maneuver to get behind the ball and continuously drive it forward until the play is reset.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

Our defensive strategy is built upon 3 foundational pillars. The defender verifies its position strictly within our designated field limits using the ultrasonic sensors. Then, it applies the compass-driven PD controller to its motors to correct them and maintain a strict forward-facing alignment. Finally, it acts as a blocker, mirroring the ball's lateral movement to intercept shots.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

Yes, the robots communicate via the ESP-NOW protocol, allowing the defender to constantly monitor the striker's status on the field. If the striker goes out of bounds (or becomes inactive), the defender dynamically switches to the striker's behavioral logic. This strategy ensures we maintain offensive pressure and prevents the opponent from driving the ball toward our goal.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

The algorithm we are most proud of is our ball detection logic. We had been working with the TSSP4038 sensors for quite some time, but initially struggled to achieve peak efficiency. We sought inspiration from top-tier world championship teams utilizing the same hardware (such as Munako Aegis). By thoroughly analyzing their public documentation, we expanded our knowledge base and engineered a software solution capable of tracking and pursuing the ball with high efficiency.
