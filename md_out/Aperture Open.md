## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

Fusion 360 was used to design the mechanical parts. The project combines methods used by international teams and methods developed by our own.
The robot is designed in a conical format to increase the used area without creating blind spots. For quick repairs and an easier assembly process, we included a lid on the bottom that gives access to the internal area of the robot.
We changed from PLA and ABS to PETG filament, that has a higher value of tensile strength, being ideal to absorb impact.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

Supports were produced using a Bambu Lab A1 3D printer, and the printed circuit boards (PCBs) of copper and glass fiber were manufactured by JLCPCBs.
The PCBs were specifically designed to fit components, with cuts for motors, dribbler and battery.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

Four GM4108 motors allow an omnidirectional movement. In a graph of sine and cosine of an angle the incerceptation is in 45°, so between motors 90° is ideal. To include a dribbler and minimize the impact on movement, the angle was increased to 100°.
Four omniwheels are mounted with two flat parts, one with hooks to lock the subwheels wire and one with cavities for the hooks, reducing weak points.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

A small JF-0826B solenoid of 6V and 20N powered with 40V allows a stronger yet smaller kicker, occupying less space in the center of the structure. A PETG pin is connected to the solenoid plunger and was designed to pass through the dribbler. Its curved shape maximizes contact area with the ball.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

The mechanism uses two pulleys and a belt, connecting the motor to the roller with a 1:1 relation, producing less surface damage than using gears. The silicone disks of A55 shore are sanded to increase grip and have a curve to improve its contact area with the ball, focusing in control precision.

## Mechanical Innovation

*Mechanical Innovation*

To reduce the width of the dribbler, keep using the pulleys and belt system and maximize the surface area of the silicone disks, it was created a design where the pulleys enter partially in the dribbler support, with one of the silicone disks having an internal curve where the dribbler support enters to hold the roller bearing. This way, the silicone sizes were preserved and the dribbler width was reduced, decreasing the angle change of the motors and reducing the impact on the robot's movement.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

The robot uses a 12.6V, 2.2 Ah LiPo battery instead of 1.1Ah considering new higher power demand components. Converters provide 20V for the motors, 48V for the kicker, 5V for the Raspberry Pi and 3V3 for the bottom LEDs. External energy input enables battery replacement without restarting the robot.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

To drive the motors we use Field-Oriented Control algorithm with Simple FOC mini module, that translate the signal from microcontroller to Step-Up voltage, maximizing force.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

There are two Raspberry Pico 2 as microcontrollers, one being responsible for reading most of the sensors and running the main code of the robot, and the other being responsible only for the motors. It is dual core and can be programmed in Arduino IDE, an improvement when compared to the previous controller, a STM32F405. The camera processor used in the robot is the Raspberry Pi 5 of 8GB, one of the options with the higher processing capacity and RAM, allowing more ideal FPS and image quality.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The processor defines the forces needed to move in x and y axis and robot's angle. Using trigonometry, the forces are divided and sent to each motor. AS5600 absolute encoders are used to maintain the FOC algorithms and read the angular position of the motor by detecting magnetic field variations.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

To detect the ball on the field there are two cameras with 1.4mm fisheye lens that achieve 210° of Field Of View (FOV) horizontally and 150° vertically. The distance between cameras creates a blind spot that allows wires and supports to be passed without harming the vision of the field. The FOV allows only one camera to be used per time to detect the ball, reducing processing. The dribbler also uses a laser and a phototransistor to detect precisely when the ball is in position in the dribbler.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

There are 16 color sensors, each one composed of a red LED and a phototransistor. 4 Analogic to Digital Converters (ADCs) of model ADS1015 are used, each one reading four sensors. The ADCs use I2C communication, so only 2 pins, SDA and SCL, are used to send the readings to the main board.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

To obtain the angle of the robot in relation to an offset and keep track of the direction the robot is facing, there is a BNO055 sensor. It is possible to use a MPU6050 gyroscope as a second option if there are problems with the BNO, being a more familiar but older option of component. Eight VL53L1X LiDARs are used to detect distances to the walls, used to calculate the robot's position.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

The circuit is separated from the main board for safety, receives 12.6V from the battery and uses a XL6009 to convert it to 40V. When the processor sends a signal, the charging of the 6 2200uF capacitors is cut and the voltage is sent to the solenoid, with two mosfets preventing energy reversion.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

The dribbler uses an A2212 1400KV motor, meaning it can go up to 17640 RPM when powered with 12.6V, but we limit it to 20-40%, that is already enough for its use. To control the motor there is an ESC of 30A. We use a trimpot to calibrate the  sensitivity of the ball detecting system phototransistor.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

The kicker circuit uses a system of recharge and shoot to reduce the activation time and increase the shooting power. It communicates to the main board by sending a signal when the capacitors are charged and receiving a signal when to shoot. By creating a shooting logic, it is possible to minimize the activation time needed for the kicker.
To measure the charge, there is a comparator that reads the capacitors tension, and to create the logical condition there is a transistor that activates when the controller emits charge, deactivating the circuit responsible for charging the capacitors and at the same time activating the one that releases the charge and triggers the solenoid.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

The robot uses both cameras, searching for the orange threshold calibrated for the ball using HSV values. The camera that detects the ball becomes the main one and gets the ball angle in relation to the image center and its distance to the robot by relating pixels to centimeters. The data is combined with the robot's angle and position to determine the ball position on the field.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

The striker goes to the ball based on a function that relates the current ball angle read to predefined angles of movement, so the robot makes the most efficient trajectory to catch the ball. Approaching the ball, the striker slows down and activates the dribbler. The goalkeeper uses predefined positions around its own goal, going to each position based on the trajectory estimation of the ball.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

The robot's position allows movements to specific points on the field and considers an average of two calculations. One uses the 8 LiDARs and the robot's angle, getting the LiDAR rays that are closer to being perpendicular to the four walls, so they reflect back properly. The other uses the camera images, combining the angle and distance to one of the goals with the field size and robot's angle.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

The robot uses the 16 color sensors disposed in a smaller circle, that allows the robot to cross approximately 5 cm beyond the white lines, increasing the area of possible movement on the field without risks of hitting the walls. We calibrate the code to detect the white lines, and with trigonometry it is possible to find its angle in relation to the robot, that goes to the opposite direction.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

To score goals, the striker mainly relies on the movement function to get to the ball, the precision dribbler to catch and hide the ball, the kicker to shoot, and the ball and robot positions achieved with sensors. The strategy focuses on stealing and hiding the ball next to the field lines and ensuring an ideal position to shoot directly or with a curve, depending on the opponents behavior.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

To avoid the opponent team scoring, one of our robots is focused on defense. It uses the ball positions in a time interval to predict its trajectory and find, considering the position points we defined around our own goal, the ideal position to defend. Considering the rules, if the goalkeeper gets close to the ball, it uses the dribbler to catch it and changes to attack mode for a time.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

The robots communicate with each other using the HC-05 or the RCJ bluetooth modules. With it, the robots can send information during the match. By sharing the ball position and sending the striker to the other side of the field, it is possible to counter robots that hide the ball, and by sending the robots own position and specific commands, it is possible to create more dynamic strategies.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

With the FOV and cameras’ placement, most of the time only one camera is used in full quality to detect the ball, while the other one is either deactivated or used in low quality. This way, it is possible to dedicate the processing usage to only one camera. An algorithm is used to keep track of the ball angle, changing which camera is used when the ball is leaving the cameras FOV.
