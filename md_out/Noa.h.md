## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

I used Autodesk Fusion to create the design.
I designed it by repeatedly building and refining it, much like sculpting with clay.
The design took into account both strength and ease of maintenance.
I had to modify the design to accommodate the layering process of 3D printing.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

I built this using a 3D printer and a CNC milling machine. (I also made the mirrors and almost all of the circuit boards myself.)
As an exception, I used JLCBC’s PCB ordering service for the line sensor.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

My team uses nine motors across two robots.
Each robot is powered by four motors, with one motor used for the dribbling mechanism.
We conducted acceleration-based verification during the wheel design process to select the optimal material.
Motors used: Pololu 20:4:1
Geared-Motor HP 12V

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

The solenoid was positioned so as not to interfere with the motor.
The design optimizes the stroke and takes the camera's field of view into account.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

The driller adjusted the settings in 0.1mm increments to find the optimal solution.
FRP and PLA were used selectively to maintain strength.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

It uses an 11.1V battery, and the motor is powered directly by 11.1V.
The solenoid is powered by a voltage that has been boosted to 30V via a boost circuit, while the main microcontroller is powered by a voltage that has been stepped down to 5V.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

The motor driver that drives the motor is not the main microcontroller.
A sub-microcontroller connected to the main microcontroller via UART drives the motor driver.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

Teensy 4.1: Main microcontroller (integration and processing of all data)
Seeeduino XIAO RP2350*2: Motor drivers and line sensors (processing of individual tasks)

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The Teensy 4.1 sends motor speed commands to the Seeeduino via UART.
The Seeeduino uses PWM to control each motor driver based on the received commands.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

The system integrates and processes data from the four cameras mounted on the Robot.
The sensors used to determine the conditions for activating the kicker detect these conditions using LEDs and phototransistors.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

Sensors are mounted on the underside of the robot.
Although there are only four sensors mounted on the robot, this is not a problem because when they detect an obstacle, the robot avoids it based on information from the camera.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

To estimate its position, the system uses ultrasonic sensors installed in all four directions and the position of the goal obtained from camera images. We use the BNO055 to determine direction.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

When the microcontroller detects that the ball has entered the robot's intake port, it sends a current from the microcontroller to the FET.
The solenoid directly pushes the plate forward to kick the ball out.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

In the driver, the microcontroller sends an analog signal directly to the FET. From there, it controls the 11V current to power the driver.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

The analog multiplexer supports only up to 16 pins. We optimized the placement of the 16 sensors capable of detecting lines to achieve the most effective configuration.
We solved this by arranging 32 phototransistors throughout the system and processing them in pairs.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

Our robots tracking the ball color with a UnitV camera for ball recognition and sending the results to the Teensy using UART.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

We use PD control for ball tracking. Variable gain allows quick turning at wide angles and suppresses oscillation near the front. The D-term applies only during convergence to prevent overshoot. Control parameters were optimized in a simulator under extreme high-speed conditions by visualizing approach trajectories and robot disturbances.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

The robot is equipped with ultrasonic distance sensors in four directions, which it uses to move toward the center of the court if the ball cannot be found, by utilizing the distance from the court walls.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

Sixteen line sensors feed a sub-MCU that reports only sensors detecting white. Each sensor has a known mounting angle; when several fire, we take the vector average of their angles to find the boundary, then add 180° to set an escape vector away from it. If the ball is near, we blend 70% escape with 30% pursuit. Ultrasonics prevent escaping into a wall, choosing the freest direction near corners.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

We have kicker and dribbler modes. In kicker mode we chase, face the target goal, and kick once the ball sensor confirms possession; a chase-angle calc guides the ball into the intake. Aiming uses the compass far out and camera goal-edges when close (hysteresis switch). kick() aligns, briefly dribbles, then fires ~30ms. In dribbler mode we secure the ball, turn away, advance, face goal, and shoot.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

In the Vision league, robots are characterized by their smaller size compared to those in the Infrared league. Because of this, the effectiveness of defense is lower than in the Infrared league, so we have assigned both robots to offense.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

Our robots does not have communication capabilities.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

One of our robots estimates distance using machine learning. The model takes camera-detected object coordinates as input and estimates the distance to items like the ball or goal. We used a distillation approach: a neural network trained on 200+ training and 80+ validation data points, whose outputs then trained a polynomial regression model. This model runs inference on a Teensy 4.1 in about 20μs per iteration, with accuracy held to a mean absolute error under 1cm.
