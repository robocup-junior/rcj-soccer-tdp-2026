## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We designed the mechanical parts using Autodesk Fusion. We utilized the FEM of Autodesk Fusion to optimize the stiffness of each part. We performed thorough review in each component selection, including each screw to prevent the lack of fastener strength, the pressure area of the omniwheel’s roller, and the dribbler shaft, to achieve the well balanced rigidity while maximizing the maintainability.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

We used 3D printing for large and complex 3D parts, and CNC milling for aluminum parts.
The hyperbolic mirror required a special process, which combines the smooth 3D mold made by JLCPCB, applied with the heated PVC mirror to shape the mirror.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

To achieve the best agility, we chose four omniwheels each driven by maxon RE16 with 19:1 gearhead as a drive system. Four omniwheels placed every 90 degrees have a stable moving force gradient than three wheels, yet better weight performance than five or more wheels. Since no commercial omniwheel satisfies our needs, we designed a high grip silicone rubber omniwheels and built them in-house.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

We chose the Takaha CB1037 solenoid for its stroke and driving force, and applied short-duration overdrive to obtain sufficient kicking force. The kicker plane was refined through iterative design to prevent the ball from entering the holding area and to avoid off-center impacts on the ball.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

Our robot equips a cylinder shaped dribble covered with silicone rubber to grip the ball. The roller is driven by a DC motor through the gear. It has a spring loaded pivot mechanism that provides robust ball capturing, and the hard stop prevents the excessive motion that loses the stability.

## Mechanical Innovation

*Mechanical Innovation*

A standout feature of our mechanical design is the dribbler, optimized for pivot range and spring load to maximize stability. During testing, we faced issues with the robot losing control of the ball. Hypothesizing that high-frequency vibrations were causing this, we revised the pivot joint tolerances and adjusted the upper limit. This fixed the issue, resulting in a dribbling mechanism that offers exceptional stability alongside remarkable agility.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

The robot has a battery voltage of 11.1-12.6 V as a primary, 45 V as a kicker power, and 3.3 and 5 V as a logic voltage. The battery voltage is directly supplied to the motor and subsystems through the fuse. We use XL6019 for 45 V, OKL-T/6-W12N-C DC-DC for 5V, and LCD’s built-in regulator for 3.3V.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

We use two DRV8412 motor drivers which enables the speed and direction of each of the four drive motors. Motor current can be monitored by an MCU through the built-in shunt resistor. This current monitoring allows detecting the robot being stuck or pushing against another robot.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

The robot has five MCUs or modules. Our multi MCU design allocates each microcontroller to each timing critical processing such as pulse width monitoring of ultrasonic or pulse ball sensors. The system centers STM32F446RE which has 6 UARTs, providing the communication to all other MCUs. We chose PIC32MK1024MCM100 as a line sensor MCU for its larger number of inputs, OpenMV H7 as a camera MCU, and the XIAO ESP32S3 as an inter-robot communication MCU.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The main MCU STM32F446RE generates a couple of PWM signals for each motor, and is directly supplied to the DRV8412 motor driver. The motor is controlled by LAP (Locked Anti-Phase) PWM, which prevents the overshoot of the robot by inertia by braking the motor while downsloping the PWM.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

For ball detection, we developed circularly placed 24x TSSP58038 IR sensors enclosed by a 3D printed screen. The pulse width obtained from each sensor is processed by SMA over a fixed time, then 2D projected based on each sensor's mounting angle, which determines the relative direction to the ball.
We smooth the number of responding sensors with a first-order filter and use that value as an indicator of relative distance.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

The circuit detects the line by the LEDs and phototransistor pair measures the light intensity. All the voltage levels are converted to digital signals,enabling easy and high speed processing. We designed both LED and phototransistor thresholds fully adjustable via LDO and DAC for game robustness.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

The robot has three ultrasonic sensors and an omnidirectional camera for navigation. The omnidirectional camera determines the relative angle, width, and distance of blue and yellow goals. To keep the robot heading to one direction, we use Euler angles obtained from a BNO055.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

The kicker circuit uses a boost converter XL6019 capable of generating up to 48V, and stores the energy in a 2200 uF capacitor. Stored energy will be bypassed through a FET and drives solenoid, generating the force to kick the ball.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

The dribbler circuit uses a boost converter XL6019 to generate up to 28 V, and drives the dribbler motor through a TB67H450 motor driver. We limit and monitor the current  from the MCU as we apply a higher than the rated voltage. This achieves a higher speed while preventing the heat damage.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

The main improvements in our electronics are white-line sensing with comparators and LED brightness control that can be adjusted from software. The line-sensor board has 60 sensors, so reading every sensor individually with an ADC would make each cycle longer. In an oscilloscope measurement comparing the delay to a binary sensor result, the comparator output in this circuit became available about 32 us earlier than ADC readout. Across all 60 sensors, this corresponds to about 2 ms per readout cycle. At a maximum speed of 1.6 m/s, the robot travels 3 mm in 2 ms. Since the white line is 20 mm wide, this difference may affect line-detection response, so fast detection with comparators is useful. We also generate the reference voltage from the MCU's DAC, which makes threshold configurable from software. The LED brightness is also configurable by  software, enabled by LDO feedback circuit combined with a digital potentiometer. This allows both the threshold and illumination level to be changed without hardware adjustment, and can support automatic setting of the sensor reference voltage.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

We calculate the ball direction by averaging the detection pulse width from each IR sensor over a fixed time, then synthesizing those values as vectors on a plane based on the mounting angle of each sensor. We do not directly measure the distance to the ball. Instead, we smooth the number of responding sensors with a first-order filter and use it as an indicator of ball closeness.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

Strategically, a forward robot approaches the ball to capture and dribble to the opponent’s goal. The target movement angles are stored as a look-up table, read and join them as a spline curve,  enabling a smoother trajectory. The algorithm prioritizes approaching to the ball when the ball is far, and gradually biased to track the goal as the ball gets closer.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

The robot does not use absolute position estimation, but switches behavior using an omnidirectional camera and ultrasonic sensors. The camera, combined with a hyperbolic mirror, provides goal-related information for various strategies. The ultrasonic sensors measure wall distance to adjust ball movement and serve as a fallback when the goals cannot be detected.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

The robot detects the field lines by a 32x light intensity sensor placed circularly around the center, and additional sensors on the left, right, and rear edge. It calculates the closest point on the line, reverses the resulting vector, and moves along it at a constant speed to stay inside the field.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

Our algorithm keeps dribbling and kicking the ball to the opponent’s goal, not only when the ball is in the playing field, but also when the ball is outside of the playing field on either side of the opponent's goal. The color signature of the goal is used to determine the target shooting angle, enabling the dodging of the opponent's robot and increasing the scoring accuracy.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

The robot assigned the goalkeeper role defends the goal by tracing the penalty area line while moving laterally to the ball. This blocks the incoming shots, including the friendly bounceback. The goalkeeper algorithm also utilizes the color signature from the omnidirectional camera, estimating the relative position from the own goal, preventing the off-trace.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

By using communication, the robots can switch roles more flexibly, allowing them to avoid being restricted to fixed roles and to fully capitalize on scoring opportunities.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

Our software innovation is unified sequence control.
The algorithm adapts the best behavior based on the fusion data from multiple sensors, enabling it to determine which offence or defence robot approaches the goal and indicates the appropriate timing of initiating the shoot.
The color signature of each goal acquired by the omnidirectional camera plays an important role in our software, enabling the robot to find the non-blocked region of the opponent's goal and increase the scoring.
