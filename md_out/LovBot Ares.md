## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

Last year during worlds, we experienced many mechanical issues such as parts cracking from a result of heavier robots crashing into us. To fix this we came up with a much modular design. We identified components that will experience the highest amounts of collision and split that part off on SolidWorks. This made the part easy to replace mid match with only 2 screws. We split our main chassis into multiple parts according to the sensors they support to make replacing and accessing wires easier.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

We used Bambu Lab's P1S to 3D print almost the entire robot

We used JLCPCB to manufacture our PCBs

Since we implemented a front facing camera, we had to shift a few parts on our main chassis it from blocking our omnidirectional camera.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

Our team used 4 motors(JMP-BE-3561) for each robot positioned at 45, 135, 225, and 315 degrees. This provided the robots with omnidirectional movement. Our offense robot uses an additional motor (Maxon 139885) that powers the dribbler. For our wheels, we 3D printed 2 frames that clamp a metal ring that holds 32 mini wheels similar to a Dyson vacuum. This is then screwed together with 4 screws.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

Our team bought an EML-1042B solenoid and removed its core to change the design. We then designed our own case for precise fit. We hand-wound the coil with 20 AWG wire and connected it to our capacitor PCB to provide powerful energy discharge. Then we printed a kicker plate to distribute the force.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

This component is powered by a Maxon 139885 brushless motor driving a gearbox that spins a cylindrical silicone piece, which sucks the ball toward the robot. We control the motor using a Hobbywing Skywalker 40A V2 ESC, which communicates directly with the Teensy microcontroller.

## Mechanical Innovation

*Mechanical Innovation*

Self made solenoid. This allowed us to maximize the given space by designing our own kicker box system. Because of this, we were able to produce a stronger kick as we had more space to wrap more layer of copper wire. Another key part of our kicker was the capacitor system. Instead of having our capacitors directly connected on the PCB, we 3D designed a separate capacitor storage on the side of the robot allowing us to wire them to increase the produced voltage leading to a stronger kick.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

We use a LiPo 3s 2200mah battery which has 12.6V on full charge. This is connected to our switch board which connects to our motors that run on 12V. The switch board connects to our teensy shield which has a buck converter that steps down to 5V.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

We purchased two H-bridge motor controllers from AliExpress and they each drive two JMP-BE-3561 motors independently. Through arduino, we can have full control over the motor through PWM speeds.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

We have experimented using both the Teensy 4.2 and Arduino Mega. We have decided to go with the Teensy as its much faster and also takes less space. It also provides us with enough analog ports, I2C ports, and UART for our camera.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

Our Teensy 4.1 microcontroller use PWM signals to control our 4 motors. The Teensy generates high-frequency PWM outputs that are sent to the two motor controllers. They then take that to interpret the duty cycle of each signal which determines the motor speed.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

We use an OpenMV RT1062 that is positioned facing upwards into a self-made convex mirror. Using color blob detection and trigonometry, we can calculate the angle and distance of the ball. Our UnitV K210 camera is positioned above the capturing zone pointing down to increase angle accuracy when getting the ball.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

Our circular light system uses 24 photoresistor sensors wired as a voltage divider to detect changes in surface brightness. The analog signals are routed through two 8-bit multiplexers to a single analog input into the Teensy. A sliding resistor on the system allows us to adjust brightness/contrast.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

Our robot uses ultrasonic sensors on all four sides, connected to our processor via analog pins. We convert raw readings to centimeters, mapping a coordinate system of the court for ideal positioning. These readings keep the robot fully aware of its position, while maintaining 0° forward at all times.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

Our kicker module is powered by a 48V booster bought from AliExpress. Our LiPo battery is directly connected to the converter which takes the 11.1V and boosts it to a much higher voltage needed for kicking. The output voltage is stored in 4 capacitors, which rapidly discharges through a solenoid.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

Our dribbler uses a Maxon 139885 motor and a 3D printed gearbox to increase RPM. We control the motor using a Hobbywing Skywalker 40A V2 ESC, which communicates directly with the Teensy microcontroller.

*Electronics Innovations*

Our new 24 photoresistor sensors increases field coverage with earlier detection. This significantly improved our ability to detect the white lines and also allows us to calculate approach angle and perform edge-following strategies.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

We use an OpenMV camera running blob detection to locate the orange ball. It identifies the largest orange-colored blob in frame, calculates its pixel offset from center, then converts this to an angle and distance using trigonometry. This data is sent to our main processor via UART at 115200 baud each tick, where it's used to drive ball-tracking logic, essential for our offense and defense bots.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

For our around ball algorithm, the robot moves toward the ball while applying a directional offset based on which side the ball is on. This offset is calculated using an exponentially scaled ball angle ratio, a ball distance ratio, and a tuning ratio.  The offset increases rapidly as the ball moves to a wider angle and gets closer to the robot, then gradually approaches a maximum value.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

Using our 4 ultrasonic sensors, we calculate our position using a coordinate plan. By setting the middle of the field to (0,0), we calculate x and y offsets using distance to determine the most effiencent route to our desired position.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

Using our circular light sensor, we are able to detect the white lines by scanning and calculating the minimum and maximum thresholds. When white detected, the robot will return back to middle for 400ms. We also have a safe wall algorithm that uses ultrasonic sensor to form a coordinate plane and adjust direction based on field positioning.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

Once we capture the ball, we determine what strategy to perform using our ultrasonic sensors. If our robot is far back, we set target to aim at the goal. If we are near the side walls, we hide the ball and travel parallel to the wall. If the ball gets stuck outside the white line, we use our front camera to accuratly face and capture the ball, then exucute a strategy based on position on field.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

Our defender uses ultrasonic-based coordinate-aided positioning to hold a goal-line position. It models ball motion as a velocity vector to predict the goal-line intercept, enabling anticipatory repositioning. The robot computes angles to align itself collinear between the ball and goal, sliding laterally until optimally positioned, with dedicated handling for wall-kicks and corners.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

We did not implement any communication system between our offense and defense robot

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

Our ball trajectory system improves on simply reacting to the ball's instantaneous angle — which compounds small errors into large misses on diagonal shots. Instead, our robot calculates the ball's velocity and direction components each tick, predicting its exact horizontal position when it arrives at the goal line. This allows our defender to reposition anticipatively before the ball gets close, dramatically improving its save rate on long-distance diagonal shots.
