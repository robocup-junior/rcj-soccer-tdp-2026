## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We used Fusion 360 for CAD and Bambu Lab for our 3D printing. The goal for our design was to make something simple and reliable, without any sensors that could be potentially too hard to debug, such as those with I2C. 
Initially, we had many sensors and wires at the top of the robot, including the MPU and ultrasonics. We realized this would obstruct the camera and create blind spots, so we moved the camera and mirror to the very top and compacted everything else toward the bottom.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

We used the Bambu Lab 3D printer to print out our chassis and walls.
We found out that the handle was blocking our ultrasonic sensors. We moved the handle to a different place that would block the camera but wouldn’t interfere with anything else.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

Each of our two robots uses four Joinmax JMP-BE3561 12 V DC motors, giving us a total of eight motors across both robots. We chose a four-motor omnidirectional drive system because it provides smooth movement in any direction without requiring the robot to turn first. We used standard omni wheels rather than designing our own, as they provided reliable traction and maneuverability.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

We have a solenoid mounted at the front of our offense robot to generate a fast and powerful kick. We designed the mounting brackets in CAD and 3D printed them to ensure the solenoid remained rigid during repeated impacts while keeping the mechanism lightweight.

## Mechanical Innovation

*Mechanical Innovation*

For our defense robot, we chose to have the motors oriented so that the wheels are aligned with the vertical and horizontal axes. This perpendicular configuration is very useful, as our defense robot is mainly moving left and right, allowing us to have accurate movement along the x-axis. It also makes our robot much easier to program and debug, as we can split all movement into x and y vectors.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

We use a 12.6 V LiPo battery as the main power source. The battery is connected to a switch board, which distributes 12V directly to the motor drivers. The switch board also powers our Teensy shield: a buck converter steps the voltage down to 5 V to power the Teensy 4.1 and other 5 V components.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

We drive our motors using a VNH5019 H-bridge driver. Microcontroller PWM pins control speed DIR set direction. The circuit connects the battery to VIN(with a capacitor to cut spikes) and outputs directly to the motors.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

Our robot uses the Teensy 4.1 as the central brain, which handles the sensors in real-time while running the main loop logic. We chose the Teensy 4.1 because its ARM Cortex-M7 processor runs at 600 MHz, which lets us execute our localization formulas, motor directions, and functions simultaneously without any system lag.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

Our Teensy controls motors by translating vectors into wheel speeds. It sends distinct signals per motor to the driver using digital direction pins to spin forward or backward, and a PWM to control the motor speed.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

We use an OpenMV camera pointed up to a 360 degree mirror, allowing us to see the whole field. We then use color thresholds to identify orange blobs, and use the coordinates of the center of the blobs relative to the camera lens as well as size to determine the ball angle and ball distance.

## Line Detection

*How does your line detection circuits work?*

Our line detection system uses downward-facing grayscale sensors to distinguish the white boundary line from the green field. Sensors measure reflected light as an analog signal: white surfaces reflect more light (higher reading), while the green field reflects less (lower reading).

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

Our robots navigate and locate their position on the field using ultrasonic sensors, allowing the processor to calculate coordinates relative to the center of the pitch by taking the differences between opposite boundaries. To keep the direction the bot faces, a digital compass is used to keep a continuous heading from 0 to 359 degrees.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

Our kicker system uses a 48 V boost converter to step the battery voltage up to the level required by the kicker. Our capacitor stores the energy until the Teensy sends a trigger signal. The capacitor then rapidly discharges through a solenoid, producing a kick.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

Our OpenMV camera and convex mirror track the orange ball, processing data through three main functions. First, the code verifies if the ball is in view using a signal threshold. Next, it returns a 0–359° heading relative to the front bumper. Finally, it uses the ball blob's distance from the camera center to estimate range, letting the robot know whether to orbit, chase, or defend.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

Both robots track the ball using its real-time angle and distance. The striker chases it anywhere on the field, using smooth orbital arcs to loop behind it and shoot. The defender guards the goal, returning to the center when the ball is lost, and tracking it as it nears. If the ball lingers in front of the goal for too long, the defender clears it past the halfway line.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

We use the ultrasonic sensors to determine the distances to find the center of the field when the ball is not detected. For example, for the offensive robot, it uses a threshold that determines the area which it considers the center and stops when it is in that area. When it has the ball, the goalie uses our coordinate system to calculate the smallest line to the goal.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

Our robots use greyscale sensors at the bottom of our robot. When any greyscale reads a value over a certain threshold, we can tell that our robot is on the white line. The robot then uses our localization to go towards the center of the field for a short period of time.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

To score goals, our robot uses dynamic curve calculations to loop around the ball from behind. Then, we use localization algorithms to find the direction of the net, and move towards it. The kicker then used to strike the ball and give it more power to catch the opponent’s defender off guard.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

Our goalie defends the goal by moving left and right, using ultrasonic sensors to stay within bounds and reversing direction if it drifts too far. If the ball lingers in front of the net for a set period, the robot clears it across the field before returning to its original defensive position to support the offensive robot.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

Our robots currently operate independently using their own sensors. In the future, we plan to add Bluetooth communication so they can share the ball's location, broadcast roles, and request support. This connectivity will prevent both robots from chasing the ball simultaneously, allowing them to execute dynamic team strategies and switch roles based on the opponent.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

Our defense algorithm tracks X and Y coordinates to keep the robot in the optimal position to block goals and move efficiently. Inspired by real soccer goalies, it prioritizes smart positioning, tactical awareness, and clearing. By shooting the ball from behind the centerline, the defender actively handles opponents and creates scoring opportunities for our offense. This combination of efficient positioning and clearing maximizes our chances of victory.
