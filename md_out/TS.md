## Mechanical Design

*How did you design the mechanical parts of your robots?*

We used Fusion360 to design our mechanical parts. Some designs were based on our designs from previous years and inspired by other teams’ TDPs and posters from previous years. However, with the size limit decreased from our national competition and having no PCBs, we had to develop creative solutions to fit components.

## Build Method

*How did you build your design?*

3D-printed the non-electronic parts, and each circuit was hand soldered on Perfboards.

## Motors & Reason

*How many motors have you used and why?*

5 motors (four for locomotion, one for the dribbler). This provides enough force and grip to move the robot effectively without taking up excessive space.

## Kicker Design

*If your robot has a kicker, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the kicker*

The kicker utilises a 5V solenoid that receives power from 3x 4700uF capacitors that are charged by a 40v boost converter. To increase kicking power, we selected a solenoid that is rated below the voltage we are providing it, as this means that more current would flow, increasing kicking power.

## Dribbler Design

*If your robot has a dribbler, explain how you designed and built the mechanics of the dribbler.*

The drum was cast with moulding silicone / silicone sealant. The squishiness of the silicone is usually enough to prevent the ball from rebounding. The parts (layout, gear system) were designed in fusion.

## Mechanical Innovation

*Mechanical Innovation*

For our dribbler, a plain cylindrical / elliptical drum was chosen over other designs for a few reasons:
Methods of centring the ball using patterns on the drum are generally ineffective, instead the shape of the ball catching zone is used to centre the ball/
Wedge shaped dribblers have a smaller catching range and can also have anti-ball centering properties - when the ball moves to one side, the wedge spins the ball angled to that side and causes it to become stuck to that side.

## Power Circuit

*How does your power circuits work?*

The 4S battery’s 14.8V is the main source of power. From there, the battery power is split between all other modules. The boost converter boosts the voltage to 40V for our kicking circuit, while the buck converter decreases the voltage to 5V.

## Motor Drive Circuit

*How do you drive your motors? Explain the circuits you use for that*

Each motor is connected to a Steel Bar Powerful Brushless Motor Driver v4. This motor receives instructions from the Raspberry Pi through I2C, then runs a PID loop to control motor voltages.

## Microcontroller & Reason

*What kind of micro controller or board do you use for your robot? Why did you decide to use this part for your robot? If you have more than 1 processor, explain each one separately.*

The robot is centred around a Raspberry Pi 5 8GB, which was originally chosen for its high processing power and ease of use via SSH.
The MaixCAM Lite modules each have a Sipeed LicheeRV Nano board, and were chosen due to their ability to run AI models from their Neural Processing Units (NPUs). We chose the MaixCAM specifically because they were fairly affordable and compact.

## Motor Control

*How do you use your processor to move your motors?*

The Raspberry Pi 5 communicates to the motor drivers through I2C which then controls the voltage and current sent to the motors. We decided to set the motor drivers to speed mode, which means the Raspberry Pi requests a certain speed and the motor drivers will try to maintain that speed.

## Ball Detection

*How does your ball detection sensors and/or camera[s] work?*

We are using 4 MaxiCam Lite cameras arranged around our robot to see the field. Each of these cameras comes with a small board with an Neural Processing Unit (NPU), which can handle running an AI model which will select the ball and the goals. This model was trained locally on a GPU by using a hand labelled dataset.

## Navigation/Position Sensors

*What sensors do you use for navigation and how are these sensors connected to your processor? What sensors do you use to find your position in the field? What about the direction your robot faces?*

We have a BNO085 IMU which is used to calculate the heading of the robot. The robot will always start facing in the direction it needs to score towards, so it saves this orientation as forwards.
We use time-flight-sensors to measure the robot’s distance from walls, combined with the angle and distance towards goals from the camera to estimate its position in the field.

## Kicker Circuit

*How do you drive your kicker system? How does the circuit make the kicker work?*

Our kicker relies on a 5V solenoid, which is designed to fire with the same force as its 12V equivalent, but at a lower voltage. Combining this with 40V from the boost converter and 14.1mF of capacitance allows our kicks to be much more powerful than if we had continued using a 12V solenoid.

## Dribbler Circuit

*How does your dribbler system work? What components and circuits did you use to drive it?*

The dribbler is driven using the same motor and system as the movement motors. A gear reduction of roughly 8:1 is applied (after the builtin motor gearbox). This also serves to transmit power to the drum, as directly attaching our large motor didn’t fit.

## Electronics Innovation

*Electronics Innovations*

The kicker circuit, which is described in detail in a previous question, as well as the choice of solenoid, are the most significant electrical innovations in our bots. 

The largest difference in this design from previous designs is the choice of optocoupler, which was chosen to minimise switching time for slightly better thermal performance in the MOSFET, due to its low rise, fall, and delay times, as well as its ability to drive battery voltage with sufficient current when powered via logic voltage. 

This reduces the time the MOSFET spends in its linear region to reduce heat loss, as the MOSFET will have 40V at around 30A flowing through it, due to the low voltage solenoid chosen.

## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

Each of our MaixCams have a custom YOLO26 detection model which is trained on over 1000 hand annotated images in Label Studio to be able to identify the ball, as well as both goals. The model returns a rectangle around the object which is then sent to the Raspberry Pi through UART, using an efficient protocol we designed.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

The robot adjusts its speed based on the ball’s movement. If the distance to the ball isn’t closing as expected, the ball is moving away from the robot, so it speeds up to catch it. If the ball is becoming closer than expected, it is moving towards the robot, so it slows down to avoid overshooting.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

The robot doesn’t know exactly where it is within the field, however it combines multiple data points to allow it to make good decisions about movement. Most of the time, at least one of the cameras can see at least one goal, which it can use to line up its shot. Additionally, the time of flight sensors are used to find how close the nearest wall is, so that it doesn’t ever hit it.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

We primarily use the four time-of-flight distance sensors mounted on the robot to measure the distance to the wall, and stop moving in that direction if a wall is too close. Moreover, to avoid fully entering the penalty area, the robot uses its distance from the goal, calculated by the MaixCams.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

Once the attacker gains possession, the robot chooses between simply moving towards the goal or hiding the ball along the side wall - whichever is more convenient. It then aligns itself with the goal and fires the kicker, taking a shot at the goal. Additionally, the robot can attempt long-range shots, utilising our powerful kicker to its full potential.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

Our defending strategy is to stay along the penalty area line and move to stay inline with the ball and the goal. If a ball is very close to the robot, it attempts to capture the ball and attack, swapping roles with the other robot.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

Our robots use WiFi to communicate with each other. We use this to ensure there is always at least one robot defending the goal while keeping roles flexible. This means that if the goalie blocks the goal, it can run across the field to score while the other robot drops back to goalie.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

We used MaixHub to develop an AI vision model to locate field objects such as the goals and ball more efficiently than with standard edge detection algorithms. This allows the tickrate of the robot to be faster, allowing it to respond faster to changing field conditions and chase and block the ball more effectively. This also means that the robot won’t be tricked by any other object with a similar colour in its field of view as it is specifically trained on images of the goals and balls.
