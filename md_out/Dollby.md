## Ball Detection Method

*How do you find where the ball is? How do you read the data from the ball detection sensors and/or camera?*

An IR Seeker detects the active IR ball over I2C, giving a clock direction (12 = front) and a strength value (smaller = closer); we convert the direction to an angle each loop. A Pixy2 adds a second source, reporting each object's image position and size and identifying the ball by color. The IR sees all around; the camera is more precise but narrower.

## Ball Catch Algorithm

*How does your algorithm work to catch the ball? Is there a difference between your robots in how they move towards the ball? Explain the differences.*

The attacker never drives straight at the ball, which would knock it the wrong way. It adds a sideways component that grows with the ball's offset from front, curving around it until the ball sits between robot and goal. The defender is the opposite: it never collects the ball, it stays in front of it to block.

## Positioning Algorithm

*How do you use your sensors in your algorithm to find your position inside the field and how do you use that position to move your robots around?*

Instead of exact coordinates, we fuse relative references: the BNO055 IMU gives an absolute heading (a fixed "forward"), the Pixy2 gives the goal's bearing, and the IR Seeker gives the ball's bearing. From these the robot always knows its orientation and where to go. Our omni drive then moves in any direction while holding the chosen heading.

## Line Algorithm

*How does your robot find the lines to stay inside the field? What algorithms do you use to avoid going out of bounds?*

Analog floor sensors around the robot read surface brightness against a calibrated threshold to detect the white line. When sensors trigger, we take the vector sum of their mounting angles to find the line's direction, then drive the opposite way to escape. This line-escape is the highest priority, overriding everything else.

## Goal Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to score goals? How do you use your kicker and dribbler to handle the ball?*

We orbit the ball until it's directly ahead while keeping the robot aimed at the goal with the Pixy2 (IMU as fallback). The dribbler, a brushless motor with backspin, holds the ball at the front. With no possession sensor, we infer we have it when the IR Seeker reads it close and ahead, then drive forward and fire the solenoid near the goal.

## Defense Algorithm

*What algorithms do you use to avoid the opponent team scoring? How do your robots defend your own goal?*

The defender's Pixy2 faces backward to see our own goal while the IR Seeker tracks the ball. It continuously positions itself on the line between ball and goal, becoming a moving wall that blocks any shot. We hold this line close to our goal to cover the widest angle, and treat the goal-area line as a hard boundary.

## Robot Communication

*Do your robots communicate with each other? How do you use this communication to your advantage?*

No — each robot runs fully independently on its own sensors. This is deliberate: fixed roles (one attacker, one defender) make their behaviors complement each other implicitly, so they cover different areas without interfering. It's also more robust, with no link to fail. A wireless link to swap roles is a future option.

## Software Innovation

*Software Innovations*

Our proudest work is a shared omni-drive function (wheels at 60/120/240/300°) taking direction, speed and rotation, plus a minimum-power floor that fixed "spinning but stuck" motors. On top sits our ball-orbiting algorithm: born from watching the robot hit the ball the wrong way, it curves around the ball until it's between robot and goal, turning contacts into shots on goal.
